---
title: >
    Apple at 50: My journey to the Mac
date: 2026-04-01 18:01:18 -0300
---

Growing up, I hated Apple the way only an arrogant teenager could. Who in their right mind would buy an overpriced toy instead of a "real computer"? Here's how I switched to the Mac, and haven't looked back… despite some recent reservations.

---

My family's first real computer was a [286 PC compatible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intel_80286) that we got in 1991. Everyone around us who was geeky enough to have a computer at that time was also using PCs. I was too young then to know what the options were.

We had previously borrowed a [VIC-20](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/VIC-20). I threw myself at BASIC, but quickly hit a limit. It was enough to convince my folks that I might go somewhere with this computer stuff, though. The 286 came with [MS-DOS 5.0](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MS-DOS#MS-DOS_5.x), [GW-BASIC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GW-BASIC), and most importantly a hard drive. It didn't hurt that it had a [VGA card](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Video_Graphics_Array) and [played](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commander_Keen) [many](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stunts_%28video_game%29) [excellent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scorched_Earth_%28video_game%29) [games](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfenstein_3D).

Fast forward to 1994: my family finally gave in to my pleading for an upgrade. That summer we got a [486DX2 running at 66 MHz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/I486) and loaded with 8 megabytes of RAM. I *loved* this machine. It ran [Doom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doom_%28franchise%29)!

Around this time I started to learn more about the wider computing world. I would beg for [PCWorld magazines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PC_World) whenever I saw them, and watched [Computer Chronicles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_Chronicles) religiously. I was in awe of [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun_Microsystems) and [SGI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_Graphics) machines. There was also this [NeXT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NeXT_Computer) company that seemed incredible. Turns out, [John Carmack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Carmack) used a NeXT machine to [build the Doom engine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doom_%281993_video_game%29#Engine)!

At this time, I also learned a bit about Apple's computers. They didn't impress me. Real computers, you see, had a command line. The most exciting computers ran some flavour of [UNIX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unix). These Apple machines seemed like fragile toys. I flippantly dismissed them as overpriced junk for people who didn't understand computing.

Turn forward the clock a bit further and I got access to a [VAX/VMS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OpenVMS) server through my father's school. I used a text-only version of the web for the first time, and built a "hello world" page (also text-only). I got a summer gig being a junior IT person and helped maintain a [Novell NetWare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NetWare) cluster. I got a real job building web sites, and configured web servers running [Solaris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oracle_Solaris) and [Linux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linux).

I didn't really think about Apple again until a few weeks before [Y2K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Year_2000_problem). Steve Jobs (who I mostly knew of from next NeXT at the time) was back at Apple, and [the next major version of Mac OS would be based on NeXTSTEP](https://arstechnica.com/gadgets/1999/12/macos-x-dp2/)! At this time, I was dual-booting between Windows 98 and Linux on my work machine. Imagine being able to have Photoshop and a local terminal next to each other!

It took a bit longer, but in 2002 I bought my first Mac. It was a [Power Macintosh G4 with dual 867 MHz processors and mirrored drive doors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_Mac_G4#Mirrored_Drive_Doors_%282002-2003%29). I still think it's one of the most beautiful objects Apple has ever built. I distinctly remember the "Switch" ad campaign being a deciding factor. [The one with Gianni Jacklone](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fLYKN7VBE5M) spoke to me directly. [The one with Ellen Feiss](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=c72d4-LpilM) is still a meme.

For the first couple of weeks, I worried that I'd made a terrible mistake. The machine was gobsmackingly expensive, and Mac OS X was still very new. Yes, it ran Photoshop and Microsoft Office, but it took a while for me to find replacements for some of my Windows apps and to get a feel for the OS. David Pogue's *[Mac OS X: The Missing Manual](https://archive.org/details/macosxmissingman0002pogu)* helped a lot.

But man, what an exciting time. Mac OS X felt like *the* platform for building the web and dealing with servers. This was just a bit before the web entered its ["2.0" era](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_2.0). I started reading [Daring Fireball](https://daringfireball.net/2002/08/baby_needs_a_new_pair_of_processors) just as John Gruber launched it. I followed [VersionTracker](https://web.archive.org/web/20020604001259mp_/http://www.versiontracker.com/macosx/index.shtml) and [MacUpdate](https://web.archive.org/web/20020606030057/http://www.macupdate.com/) to learn what new OS X software was available. I found out about [Panic](https://web.archive.org/web/20020923143721/http://www.panic.com/) and the great apps they continue to build. There was a real sense of community and everything felt possible.

I still love Apple's products today, but there are new caveats. [I've been complaining](https://anderegg.ca/2026/01/20/reckoning-with-liquid-glass) about some smaller-stakes areas where I feel like they've lost the plot recently. But they've also stopped being a plucky underdog on the brink of bankruptcy, and started being a world power making [a series](https://appleinchina.com/) [of terrible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/App_Store_%28Apple%29#Antitrust_allegations) [choices](https://www.macrumors.com/2025/08/07/tim-cook-gift-to-trump/). I think their products are still the best fit for my needs, but I can't love a megacorp the way I loved an upstart.

I've been rooting for Apple for just under half their existence, and I am still rooting for them. I can't help but be excited for [what happens next](https://www.apple.com/newsroom/2026/03/apples-worldwide-developers-conference-returns-the-week-of-june-8/). Here's hoping the next 50 years have even more of that early-2000s magic that made me switch.
